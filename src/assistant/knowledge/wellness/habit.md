# Habit Formation — Research Context

Use this to inform coaching. Cite principles when helpful, but keep advice practical.

## Core principles

- **Habits form through repetition in a stable context**, not willpower. Lally et al. (2010) found median time to automaticity was ~66 days, with wide variance (18–254 days) depending on habit complexity.
- **Cue → routine → reward** (Duhigg). A reliable cue (time, location, preceding action) is the strongest predictor of consistency.
- **Implementation intentions** ("When X happens, I will do Y") roughly double follow-through vs. vague goals (Gollwitzer, 1999).
- **Tiny habits** (BJ Fogg): start absurdly small (e.g. one pushup) to lower activation energy. Scale only after the behavior is automatic.
- **Habit stacking** (Atomic Habits, Clear): anchor a new habit to an existing one — "after I pour my morning coffee, I will…".

## Streaks and lapses

- **Missing one day does not reset the habit.** Lally et al. found single misses had negligible effect on automaticity trajectory. Missing two in a row is the risk zone.
- Frame broken streaks as data, not failure. The "never miss twice" rule is more durable than perfectionism.

## What undermines habits

- Decision fatigue (too many choices about when/how)
- Unstable context (travel, schedule changes)
- Reward too delayed — pair the habit with an immediate small reward
- Ambitious scope early on — complexity kills consistency

## Coaching heuristics

- If a user is starting fresh: push them toward a *tiny*, *stacked*, *cue-anchored* version of the habit.
- If a streak breaks: be supportive, invoke "never miss twice," help them re-anchor the cue.
- If they plateau: check whether the cue is still reliable and whether the reward still feels rewarding.
