# Study Techniques — Research Context

Use this to help the user study effectively, not just "more."

## High-impact techniques (Dunlosky et al., 2013 meta-review)

Ranked by evidence of effectiveness across subjects and ages:

**High utility**:
- **Retrieval practice**: closed-book self-testing. Quizzing yourself beats rereading by a wide margin.
- **Distributed practice (spacing)**: spreading study over days/weeks produces 2–3× better long-term retention than cramming the same total time.

**Moderate utility**:
- **Elaborative interrogation**: asking "why is this true?" while studying.
- **Self-explanation**: explaining steps to yourself out loud.
- **Interleaved practice**: mixing problem types within a session (vs. blocking). Feels harder, produces better transfer.

**Low utility (despite popularity)**:
- **Highlighting and underlining** — feels productive, weak effect.
- **Rereading** — passive, minimal retention gain.
- **Summarization** — only effective if well-trained; otherwise noise.
- **Keyword mnemonics / imagery** — narrow applicability.

## Cramming vs. spacing

- **Cramming works for short-term recall** (next-day exam) but produces rapid forgetting.
- **Spacing** — same total study time distributed over more sessions — produces dramatically better retention at 1 week+.
- For a 10-hour total study budget for an exam in 2 weeks: 1 hour/day × 10 days >> 5 hours × 2 days the night before.

## Exam preparation

- **Practice under test conditions**: same time limit, no notes, same format. This engages retrieval and simulates pressure.
- **Error log**: track missed questions → review concepts tied to errors. Most efficient use of review time.
- **Sleep before exams** matters more than last-minute cramming. A full night of sleep produces better recall than 2 extra hours of review.

## Focus and session design

- **Pomodoro** (25 min focus, 5 min break) is popular but not uniquely research-backed. Any cadence that sustains deep focus works.
- **Single-tasking** during study. Phones in another room — even silent phones on the desk measurably reduce cognitive capacity (Ward et al., 2017).
- **Deliberate practice**: work at the edge of your ability, with feedback. Studying what you already know is comfort, not progress.

## Coaching heuristics

- If the user crams: introduce distributed practice and retrieval, even modestly.
- If they highlight heavily: suggest replacing 50% of highlighting time with self-quizzing.
- If they procrastinate: start with a tiny commitment (10 min). Starting is the hardest part.
- If exam is soon: focus on retrieval of weak areas via error log, not rereading strong areas.
- Protect sleep around exams — tell the sleep agent to prioritize.
