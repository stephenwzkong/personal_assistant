# Long-Term Goal Setting — Research Context

Use this to help users set durable, achievable goals — not just aspirational ones.

## Goal-setting frameworks

- **SMART** (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) — baseline structure. Most "goals" users state fail the M or T test.
- **OKRs** (Objectives + Key Results): qualitative ambition paired with 3–5 quantitative, measurable key results. Originated at Intel, popularized at Google. Good for quarterly cycles.
- **WOOP** (Wish, Outcome, Obstacle, Plan) — Gabriele Oettingen's research. Mental contrasting: imagine the goal, imagine the obstacles, make an if-then plan. Outperforms pure positive visualization (which is associated with *worse* outcomes — "fantasy" saps motivation).
- **BHAG** (Big Hairy Audacious Goal) — Collins & Porras. Useful at the 10+ year horizon, but must be broken into concrete milestones.

## Research findings

- **Specificity and difficulty**: Locke & Latham's 35-year research program — specific, difficult goals produce higher performance than "do your best" goals, provided the person is committed and has the ability.
- **Written goals**: Matthews (2015) found people who wrote goals and shared weekly progress with a friend achieved ~76% vs. ~43% for those who only thought about them.
- **Implementation intentions** ("If X happens, I will do Y") roughly double follow-through rates.
- **Fresh start effect**: people are more likely to pursue goals after temporal landmarks (new year, birthday, month start). Leverage this for re-commitment.

## Milestone design

- **Break goals into milestones reachable in 2–8 weeks.** Longer horizons lose motivational pull; shorter ones feel trivial.
- **Process goals > outcome goals** for habits and skills ("write 500 words daily" vs. "publish a book"). Outcome goals depend on factors outside your control.
- **Leading indicators** (behaviors you can control) predict lagging indicators (outcomes). Track leading.

## Common failure modes

- **Too many goals**: pursuing 5+ major goals simultaneously produces worse outcomes than focusing on 1–2.
  - **Jobs' "rule of three"**: Steve Jobs famously committed to no more than three priorities at once — finish them, then pick the next three. The hard part is saying no to the 4th. Use this as an anti-overwhelm default when a user is juggling too much.
- **Vague definitions of done**: "get in shape" vs. "bench 1.5× bodyweight by October."
- **No review cadence**: goals without weekly/monthly review drift into forgotten aspirations.
- **Identity mismatch**: goals that conflict with self-image fail. Framing as "I am a person who X" is more durable than "I want to X."

## Coaching heuristics

- If the user states a vague ambition: push for concrete milestones and a review cadence before anything else.
- If they have many goals: help them rank and defer. Sequential > parallel.
- If a goal keeps slipping: check whether the obstacle is ability, motivation, or environment — each needs different interventions.
- Connect goals to related domains (fitness agent for fitness goals, reading for learning goals) so progress data lives where the work happens.
