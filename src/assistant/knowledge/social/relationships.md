# Relationships & Social Maintenance — Research Context

Use this to inform reminder cadence and coaching — not to prescribe social scripts.

## How relationships decay

- **Relationships require active maintenance.** Without contact, closeness erodes on a predictable curve. The rate varies by relationship type.
- **Hall (2019)** — it takes roughly 50 hours of time together to move from acquaintance to casual friend, ~90 hours to friend, and ~200+ hours to close friend. Time is the raw material.
- **Dunbar's number** (~150) is the cognitive ceiling for stable social relationships. Within that: ~5 intimate, ~15 close, ~50 meaningful, ~150 stable. Most people have too many acquaintances and too few intentional closer ties.

## Maintenance cadence (heuristics, not rules)

- **Immediate family / partner**: daily to multiple times per week
- **Close friends**: every 1–2 weeks to stay warm; monthly minimum before decay
- **Good friends**: monthly check-in, quarterly deeper hang
- **Distant friends / old connections**: 2–4× per year to maintain baseline
- **Weak ties / acquaintances**: annual or as context arises

These are defaults — adjust based on the actual relationship history and the user's stated norms.

## What makes contact effective

- **Quality over quantity**: one 45-min focused conversation beats five superficial texts.
- **Shared activity** produces closeness faster than just talking — especially for men (research on "side-by-side" vs. "face-to-face" preferences).
- **Responsiveness**: feeling heard and understood predicts closeness more than agreement or advice (Reis & Shaver, 1988).
- **Asymmetric effort signals**: the person who reaches out consistently often feels the relationship more than the one who just responds. Balance matters over time.
- **Vulnerability graduates**: gradually sharing more personal content deepens trust, when reciprocated.

## Common pitfalls

- **"I'll text them soon"** — the soft intention that never happens. Scheduled reminders beat vague plans.
- **Only reaching out when you need something** — relationships feel transactional and erode.
- **Avoiding reconnection after long gaps** out of embarrassment. Research shows reconnections are almost always better received than the anxious person predicts (Liu et al., 2022).
- **Over-reliance on group contexts** — group hangs are efficient but don't replace 1:1 time for close ties.

## Coaching heuristics

- If the user asks who to reach out to: prioritize overdue close ties over many acquaintances.
- If they're anxious about a long gap: reassure them — the research says the other person is almost always glad to hear from them.
- If they maintain many weak ties but few close ones: suggest rebalancing — depth beats breadth for wellbeing.
- Suggest concrete next actions (a specific time, a specific question) over vague "reach out soon."
