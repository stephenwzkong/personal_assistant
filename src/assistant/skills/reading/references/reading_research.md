# Reading & Retention — Research Context

Use this to coach on comprehension and retention, not just "finishing books."

## Reading for retention

- **Passive rereading is weak.** Highlighting and rereading feel productive but produce poor long-term retention (Dunlosky et al., 2013 meta-review).
- **Active recall / retrieval practice** is the single highest-impact study technique. After each chapter, close the book and write what you remember.
- **Spaced repetition**: reviewing material at expanding intervals (1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 2 weeks…) produces dramatically better retention than cramming. Tools like Anki automate this.
- **Elaborative interrogation**: ask "why is this true?" and "how does this connect to what I know?" while reading. Generates durable understanding.

## Active reading techniques

- **SQ3R** (Survey, Question, Read, Recite, Review) — classic method, still backed by research.
- **Cornell Notes**: split page into cues, notes, summary. Forces synthesis.
- **Feynman Technique**: explain the concept simply, in your own words, as if teaching a beginner. Exposes gaps fast.
- **Marginalia + index**: write short reactions in margins, then build a personal index on the last page (Mortimer Adler, "How to Read a Book").

## Reading strategy by book type

- **Dense nonfiction** (textbooks, technical): slow, with notes and recall. Don't rush.
- **Narrative nonfiction / history**: moderate pace, focus on arguments and evidence, not memorizing facts.
- **Fiction**: pace with enjoyment. Retention matters less than experience.
- **Skimmable nonfiction** (business, self-help): often 20% of the book contains 80% of the value. Skimming with targeted deep reads is efficient.

## Habit and pacing

- **Reading volume correlates with time allocated, not reading speed.** Speed-reading research (Rayner, 2016) shows comprehension degrades sharply above ~400 wpm for normal material.
- **20–30 min daily** compounds to ~20+ books/year at average pace. Consistency beats intensity.
- **Multiple books simultaneously** (1 fiction + 1 nonfiction + 1 reference) reduces stall risk — switch based on mood.
- **Abandonment is allowed.** Sunk cost on a bad book is still a sunk cost. ~50-page rule: if you're not engaged by then, drop it.

## Coaching heuristics

- If the user finishes books but retains little: introduce active recall and Cornell notes.
- If they stall on long books: suggest shorter sessions and a companion lighter read.
- If they have a big reading goal: focus on daily minutes rather than "X books by Y date."
- Celebrate completions but also celebrate *abandonment* of bad books — it's a skill.
